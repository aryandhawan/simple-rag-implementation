# Hallucination

Hallucination in the context of large language models refers to the generation of outputs that are factually incorrect, fabricated, or unsupported by the model's input context, produced with apparent confidence. The term is borrowed loosely from psychology, where hallucinations describe perceptions without a real stimulus. In AI, it captures the phenomenon of a model asserting false information as if it were true — inventing citations, misattributing quotes, fabricating statistics, or describing events that never occurred — without any indication of uncertainty.

Hallucination arises from the fundamental nature of how language models are trained. During pre-training, the model learns to predict the next token given its context by optimizing for statistical plausibility over a large text corpus. It learns that certain phrases follow certain patterns and that factual-sounding statements tend to appear in particular formats. However, the model does not learn to distinguish between information it genuinely "knows" (well-represented in training data) and information it is confabulating. The same token-prediction process that produces accurate factual recall can also generate grammatically fluent but factually wrong continuations.

Hallucination is classified along several dimensions. Intrinsic hallucination refers to outputs that directly contradict information provided in the model's input context — for example, a summarization model inventing a figure that appears nowhere in the original document. Extrinsic hallucination refers to outputs that add information not present in the source, which may or may not be true but cannot be verified from what was provided. Faithfulness hallucination is particularly dangerous in summarization and extraction tasks, where users expect the model's output to be strictly grounded in the source.

Several factors influence hallucination rates. Models with larger parameter counts and more diverse training data tend to hallucinate less on common facts. Fine-tuning on instruction-following data with a strong emphasis on accuracy and uncertainty expression can reduce hallucination. Retrieval-augmented generation reduces factual hallucination by supplying the model with relevant passages at inference time, grounding its response in verifiable text; however, models can still fail to faithfully use retrieved content, or the retrieved content itself may be wrong.

Detection of hallucination is an active research area. Automated fact-checking approaches compare model claims against knowledge bases or retrieve evidence from search engines. Consistency-based detection samples multiple responses to the same question and flags claims that vary across samples. Natural language inference models can score whether a claim is entailed by, neutral to, or contradicted by a reference document, enabling automated faithfulness evaluation in RAG and summarization pipelines.

Uncertainty calibration is a related property: a well-calibrated model should express lower confidence in claims that are more likely to be wrong. Eliciting uncertainty from language models — through verbal hedges ("I'm not certain, but..."), probability estimates, or structured responses indicating confidence levels — can help users identify outputs that require verification.
