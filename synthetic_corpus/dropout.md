# Dropout

Dropout is a regularization technique for neural networks that reduces overfitting by randomly deactivating a fraction of neurons during each training step. Proposed by Srivastava et al. in 2014, dropout was motivated by an analogy to ensemble learning: if many different model architectures could be averaged together, the ensemble would generalize better than any individual model. Dropout approximates this by training a different random subnetwork at each step, effectively averaging an exponentially large number of models at a fraction of the cost.

The mechanism is simple. During the forward pass at training time, each neuron is independently set to zero with probability p (the dropout rate) and kept with probability 1 − p. The retained neurons' activations are scaled up by a factor of 1/(1 − p) to preserve the expected total activation magnitude, a variant called inverted dropout. This scaling ensures that when dropout is turned off at inference time, the network's output scale is unchanged and no correction is needed. At inference, all neurons are active, and their contributions are used in full.

The typical dropout rate for fully connected layers is p = 0.5, while lower rates of 0.1 to 0.2 are common for convolutional layers, attention layers, and the inputs to recurrent networks. The appropriate rate depends on the degree of overfitting observed: a model that is clearly overfitting (large gap between training and validation loss) benefits from higher dropout, while a model that is already underfitting should use minimal or no dropout.

The regularization effect of dropout can be understood intuitively. Because any neuron might be absent on any given forward pass, the network cannot rely on any single co-activation pattern of neurons to represent a feature. It is forced to learn redundant, distributed representations that remain useful even when some neurons are missing. This redundancy improves generalization to unseen data.

In the Transformer architecture, dropout is applied at several points: to the attention weights after the softmax, to the outputs of the feed-forward sub-layers, and to the residual streams. The dropout rate for large language model training is typically lower than in classical deep learning — often between 0.0 and 0.1 — because the large amount of training data itself provides strong implicit regularization. Some large-scale pre-training recipes omit dropout entirely, relying on data diversity and early stopping instead.

Monte Carlo dropout is an application of dropout at inference time to estimate uncertainty. By performing multiple forward passes with dropout enabled and treating the variance of the resulting predictions as a proxy for model uncertainty, practitioners can quantify how confident the network is in its output. This technique is used in Bayesian deep learning contexts where uncertainty estimation is important, such as medical diagnosis and safety-critical decision systems.
