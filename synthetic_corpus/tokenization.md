# Tokenization

Tokenization is the process of converting raw text into a sequence of discrete units called tokens, which serve as the fundamental inputs to language models. Because neural networks operate on numerical vectors rather than characters or words, tokenization acts as the bridge between human-readable text and the representations the model can process. The choice of tokenization strategy significantly affects a model's vocabulary size, its handling of rare words, and its efficiency on different languages.

Early natural language processing systems tokenized text by splitting on whitespace and punctuation, treating each word as a token. This approach is intuitive but suffers from a combinatorial explosion in vocabulary size when accounting for word inflections, compound words, and proper nouns across multiple languages. A vocabulary of whole words would need to be enormous to cover most text without resorting to an unknown-word token, which discards information.

Subword tokenization methods address this by decomposing words into smaller, reusable pieces. Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), the algorithm used by GPT models and many others, starts with a vocabulary of individual characters (or bytes) and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens into a new token. This process continues until the vocabulary reaches a target size, typically 32,000 to 100,000 tokens. The result is a vocabulary where common words appear as single tokens while rare words are split into familiar sub-pieces, achieving a compact and flexible representation.

WordPiece, used by BERT, operates similarly but selects merges based on a likelihood criterion rather than raw frequency — it merges the pair that most increases the language model likelihood of the training data. SentencePiece is a language-independent tokenization library that can apply BPE or Unigram tokenization directly to raw Unicode text without requiring pre-tokenization steps like whitespace splitting, making it well-suited for multilingual models.

The tokenization of a string is not unique and can be non-obvious. A single English word may map to one token or several depending on how common it is. Numbers are often split digit-by-digit or into small numeric substrings. Programming language syntax, mathematical expressions, and non-Latin scripts tend to tokenize less efficiently than everyday English prose, leading to longer token sequences for the same amount of information. This inefficiency has practical consequences: models charged per token are more expensive for code or multilingual content, and the model's context window is consumed more quickly.

The vocabulary is stored as a lookup table mapping token strings to integer IDs, which are then converted to embedding vectors before being passed into the model. Tokenization is typically performed by a separate pre-processing step, and the tokenizer must be matched exactly to the model it was trained with — using the wrong tokenizer produces garbage inputs. Modern tokenizer implementations, such as those in the Hugging Face Tokenizers library, are written in Rust for high throughput and support fast batch processing of large corpora.
