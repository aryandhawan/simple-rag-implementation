# Overfitting

Overfitting occurs when a machine learning model learns to represent the training data so precisely — including its noise and idiosyncratic details — that it fails to generalize to new, unseen examples. An overfitted model achieves very low error on training examples but performs significantly worse on held-out validation or test data. This gap between training and validation performance, called the generalization gap, is the diagnostic signature of overfitting and the central problem that regularization techniques are designed to combat.

The mechanism of overfitting can be understood through the bias-variance trade-off. A model with low capacity (few parameters) has high bias: it makes strong assumptions that may not hold, producing systematic errors on both training and test data (underfitting). A model with high capacity (many parameters) has low bias but high variance: it can fit the training data very accurately, but its predictions are highly sensitive to the specific training examples seen and generalize poorly. Overfitting is the high-variance regime where the model has fit the noise in the training data rather than the underlying pattern.

A classic diagnostic tool for overfitting is the learning curve: plots of training and validation loss as a function of training time or dataset size. In an overfitting regime, training loss continues to decrease while validation loss begins to increase — the model is becoming more specialized to the training set at the expense of generalizability. Early stopping halts training when the validation loss stops improving and begins to rise, returning the model weights from the epoch of best validation performance rather than the final training epoch.

Regularization techniques introduce constraints or penalties that discourage the model from fitting the training data too precisely. L2 regularization (weight decay) adds a penalty proportional to the squared magnitude of the model's weights to the training loss, discouraging large weight values that often accompany overfitting. L1 regularization adds a penalty proportional to the absolute magnitude of weights, which has the additional effect of inducing sparsity. Dropout deactivates random neurons during each training step, preventing the model from relying on any particular set of co-activations.

Data augmentation reduces overfitting by artificially expanding the training dataset. For image models, common augmentations include random cropping, flipping, rotation, and color jitter; for text, augmentations include synonym replacement, back-translation, and sentence shuffling. Each augmented example is a new, slightly modified version of an existing training example, exposing the model to a broader range of variations and reducing its ability to memorize specific training instances.

Cross-validation is a technique for estimating a model's generalization performance when the dataset is too small to hold out a dedicated validation set. The training data is split into k equal-sized folds; the model is trained k times, each time using k − 1 folds for training and the remaining fold for validation. The average validation performance across all k runs provides a more reliable estimate of generalization than a single train-validation split, especially in the small-data regime where overfitting is most severe.
