# Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that enhances large language models by supplying them with relevant external documents at inference time, rather than relying solely on the knowledge encoded in their parameters during training. The core motivation is that language models have fixed knowledge cutoffs and can hallucinate facts that were never in their training data. RAG addresses both problems by retrieving up-to-date, verifiable context from an external corpus and placing it into the model's context window before asking it to generate a response.

A RAG pipeline consists of two main stages: retrieval and generation. During retrieval, the user's query is encoded into an embedding vector, which is then compared against a pre-indexed collection of document embeddings using approximate nearest-neighbor search. The top-k most similar documents or passages are retrieved from this index. During generation, the retrieved passages are concatenated with the original query — typically following a structured prompt template — and passed to the language model, which synthesizes a response grounded in the retrieved content.

The quality of a RAG system depends heavily on the indexing strategy. Documents are typically split into smaller chunks before being embedded, since embedding models have limited input lengths and smaller chunks tend to carry more focused semantic content. Common chunking strategies include fixed-size windows with overlap, sentence-boundary splits, and recursive character splitting. The choice of chunk size significantly impacts both retrieval precision and the coherence of retrieved passages.

Embedding model selection is another critical factor. The embedding model must be trained on data similar to the target domain to produce meaningful similarity scores. Models are often evaluated on benchmarks like BEIR or MTEB before being deployed in a RAG system. Dense retrieval with embedding similarity is sometimes combined with sparse retrieval methods like BM25 in a hybrid search setup, which can improve recall for queries that contain rare or technical keywords.

Reranking is frequently applied as a second-stage filter after initial retrieval. A cross-encoder reranking model scores each retrieved passage against the query more carefully than the initial embedding similarity allowed, promoting the most relevant passages to the top before they are passed to the generator. This two-stage approach balances efficiency — the fast but approximate first-stage retrieval — with precision — the slower but more accurate reranker.

Evaluation of RAG systems typically measures both retrieval quality (recall and precision at k) and generation quality (faithfulness to retrieved passages, answer correctness, and groundedness). Frameworks like RAGAS provide automated metrics that decompose these concerns, enabling systematic comparison of different pipeline configurations.
