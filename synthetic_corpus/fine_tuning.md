# Fine-Tuning

Fine-tuning is the process of continuing to train a pre-trained model on a smaller, task-specific dataset so that its parameters are adjusted to perform better on that particular task or domain. Rather than training a model from scratch — which requires enormous amounts of data and compute — fine-tuning leverages the general representations learned during pre-training and specializes them for a target application. This approach is the cornerstone of transfer learning in natural language processing.

In the standard fine-tuning workflow for language models, the pre-trained weights are loaded and then updated through additional gradient descent steps on labeled examples. For classification tasks, a task-specific head (typically a small linear layer) is added on top of the model, and both the new head and the underlying model weights are trained jointly. For generative tasks, the model is fine-tuned on input-output pairs formatted as continuation sequences, teaching it to produce the desired output style and content.

Full fine-tuning updates all of the model's parameters, which gives maximum flexibility but requires storing and optimizing a full copy of the model's gradients and optimizer states. For a model with billions of parameters, this becomes prohibitively expensive in terms of GPU memory. A 7-billion-parameter model stored in 32-bit floating point occupies roughly 28 GB just for its weights, and the optimizer states during training can triple or quadruple this memory requirement.

Instruction fine-tuning is a particularly important variant in which the model is trained on a large collection of diverse (instruction, response) pairs, teaching it to follow natural language instructions across many different task types. Models like InstructGPT and Llama-2-chat were produced through instruction fine-tuning, often combined with reinforcement learning from human feedback. This process dramatically improves the model's ability to behave as a useful assistant.

Catastrophic forgetting is a well-documented risk in fine-tuning: aggressive training on a narrow dataset can cause the model to lose the general capabilities it acquired during pre-training. Techniques to mitigate this include using a low learning rate, training for fewer epochs, applying regularization toward the original weights, or mixing in a proportion of general-purpose data alongside the task-specific examples.

Evaluation after fine-tuning should measure both task-specific performance and general capability retention. Benchmarks covering the original pre-training distribution can detect regression, while held-out examples from the target task measure the desired improvement. Logging training loss curves and validation metrics at regular intervals helps detect overfitting, which is especially common when fine-tuning on small datasets.
