# RLHF

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is a training methodology that uses human preference judgments to fine-tune language models toward producing outputs that are more helpful, harmless, and honest. The technique was popularized through its use in InstructGPT and subsequently became central to the development of ChatGPT and similar conversational AI systems. RLHF bridges the gap between the pre-training objective — predicting the next token — and the harder-to-formalize goal of generating responses that humans actually prefer.

The standard RLHF pipeline consists of three stages. The first stage is supervised fine-tuning (SFT), in which the base pre-trained model is fine-tuned on a dataset of high-quality demonstrations — example prompts paired with ideal responses written by human annotators. This produces an SFT model that has learned the general format and style of helpful responses.

The second stage trains a reward model. Human annotators are presented with a prompt and several candidate responses generated by the SFT model and asked to rank them from best to worst. These preference comparisons are used to train a separate model — typically initialized from the SFT model with a scalar output head — to predict the quality score of any (prompt, response) pair. The reward model learns to assign higher scores to responses that annotators preferred, capturing a proxy for human judgment.

The third stage applies reinforcement learning — specifically the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm — to optimize the SFT model's policy using the reward model as the reward signal. For each prompt, the policy (the language model being trained) generates a response, which is scored by the reward model. PPO updates the policy's weights to increase the probability of high-reward responses while penalizing large deviations from the SFT model through a KL-divergence penalty. This KL constraint prevents the policy from exploiting the reward model with degenerate outputs that score highly but are not actually good.

A key challenge in RLHF is reward hacking: the policy may discover response patterns that exploit weaknesses in the reward model, achieving high predicted scores without actually being more helpful. For example, models have been observed to produce confidently-worded but incorrect answers, or to generate verbose responses that superficially signal thoroughness. The KL penalty and careful reward model design mitigate but do not eliminate this risk.

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is a more recent alternative to the full PPO-based RLHF pipeline. DPO re-frames the RLHF objective as a supervised classification problem over preference pairs, eliminating the need for a separate reward model and the complexity of RL training. Despite its simplicity, DPO achieves comparable or superior results to PPO-based RLHF on many benchmarks, making it a popular choice for alignment fine-tuning.
