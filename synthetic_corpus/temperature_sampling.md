# Temperature Sampling

Temperature sampling is a method for controlling the randomness of text generation in language models by adjusting the probability distribution over the vocabulary before drawing a sample at each decoding step. Rather than deterministically selecting the highest-probability token (greedy decoding) or sampling directly from the raw model distribution, temperature sampling reshapes the distribution using a scalar hyperparameter T — the temperature — that governs how "peaked" or "flat" the sampling probabilities become.

The operation is straightforward. At each step, the language model produces a vector of logits — unnormalized scores for every token in the vocabulary. Normally, these logits are passed through a softmax to produce a probability distribution. With temperature sampling, each logit is divided by T before the softmax is applied: softmax(logits / T). When T = 1, the distribution is unchanged from the model's original output. When T < 1, dividing by a small number sharpens the distribution, concentrating probability mass on the highest-scoring tokens and making generation more deterministic and conservative. When T > 1, dividing by a large number flattens the distribution, spreading probability more evenly across the vocabulary and making generation more random and diverse.

Setting T close to zero approximates greedy decoding. The highest-logit token receives nearly all the probability mass, and the model almost always selects the most likely continuation. This setting is appropriate when consistency and accuracy are paramount — for example, in question answering where there is a single correct factual response. Setting T around 0.7 to 0.9 is common for creative writing or conversational responses, producing text that is varied and natural without becoming incoherent.

At very high temperatures (T > 1.5), the model begins sampling from tokens that it considers quite unlikely, which introduces grammatical errors, topic drift, and semantic incoherence. The temperature therefore acts as a dial between determinism and creativity, and practitioners typically tune it empirically for each application.

Top-p sampling (nucleus sampling) is closely related and is often used together with temperature. After applying temperature scaling, top-p truncates the vocabulary to the smallest subset of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds a threshold p, then samples uniformly from this nucleus. This prevents the model from generating tokens with negligible probability even when a high temperature flattens the distribution. A common configuration is temperature 0.8 with top-p 0.95.

Top-k sampling is another complementary technique that restricts sampling to the k tokens with the highest probabilities, discarding the rest. Unlike top-p, the nucleus size is fixed at k regardless of how concentrated or diffuse the distribution is. Top-k = 50 combined with temperature around 1.0 was the default in early GPT-2 generation examples and remains widely used.
