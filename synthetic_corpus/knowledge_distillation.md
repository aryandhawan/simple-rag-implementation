# Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillation is a model compression technique in which a smaller "student" model is trained to mimic the behavior of a larger, more capable "teacher" model. The goal is to transfer the generalization capability of the teacher into a student that requires significantly less compute and memory at inference time, making it practical to deploy in latency-sensitive or resource-constrained environments. Distillation was introduced by Geoffrey Hinton and colleagues in 2015 and has since become a standard tool in the machine learning practitioner's toolkit.

The core insight behind distillation is that a trained model's output probability distribution over all classes — not just the argmax class — carries rich information about the relationships between outputs. For a classification task, the teacher might assign a small but nonzero probability to incorrect classes that are semantically similar to the correct one. For example, a model classifying animal images might assign "dog" a 75% probability and "wolf" a 20% probability for an image of a wolf, reflecting genuine uncertainty and structural knowledge about the similarity between the two categories. Training the student to match these "soft labels" provides a richer learning signal than training on the one-hot hard labels alone.

The distillation training objective typically combines two terms: the standard cross-entropy loss on the hard labels (the ground-truth targets), and a distillation loss that measures the divergence between the student's output distribution and the teacher's soft probability distribution. The soft probabilities are usually computed at an elevated temperature (following the same temperature-scaling operation used in temperature sampling) so that the small probabilities on non-correct classes are amplified and more informative. A hyperparameter α controls the relative weighting of the two loss terms.

Intermediate-layer distillation extends beyond matching the final output distribution. In this approach, the student is also trained to match the hidden-state representations, attention maps, or gradients of specific intermediate layers of the teacher. This provides guidance about the teacher's internal processing, not just its final decision, and can accelerate the student's training convergence. TinyBERT is a prominent example: it distills both attention matrices and hidden-state representations from BERT at multiple layers, producing a model 7.5 times smaller than BERT-base while retaining over 96% of its performance.

Task-specific distillation first fine-tunes the teacher on the target task and then distills from that fine-tuned teacher rather than the base pre-trained model. This approach focuses the student's learning on the task-relevant behaviors of the teacher. In contrast, task-agnostic distillation trains the student to mimic the teacher on general text, producing a compact general-purpose model that can then be fine-tuned on downstream tasks.

The student architecture does not need to mirror the teacher's architecture. A teacher that is a deep Transformer can distill into a student with fewer layers, smaller hidden dimensions, or even a different architecture class entirely, such as a recurrent network or a state-space model. The student simply needs to produce outputs that match the teacher's well enough, regardless of how it achieves them internally.
