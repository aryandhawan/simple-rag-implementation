# Beam Search

Beam search is a heuristic search algorithm used during the decoding stage of sequence generation models to produce output sequences that are approximately optimal under the model's probability distribution. Unlike greedy decoding — which selects the single highest-probability token at each step — beam search maintains multiple candidate sequences simultaneously and explores a broader portion of the output space, often producing higher-quality results on structured generation tasks.

The algorithm operates by maintaining a fixed-size set of candidate sequences called the beam, parameterized by the beam width (also called beam size, denoted k). At each decoding step, every sequence in the current beam is extended by all possible next tokens, generating k × |V| candidate extensions, where |V| is the vocabulary size. These candidates are scored by their cumulative log-probability, and the top k are retained for the next step. Sequences that generate an end-of-sequence token are moved to a completed pool and replaced in the active beam by the next-best candidate.

When the decoding process terminates — either because all beam sequences have completed or a maximum length is reached — the completed sequences are ranked by their total log-probability (often normalized by length to avoid penalizing longer sequences). The highest-scoring completed sequence is returned as the final output. Because beam search evaluates k paths at each step, it is k times more computationally expensive than greedy decoding, but it systematically avoids many of the locally suboptimal token choices that greedy decoding commits to irrevocably.

Length normalization is a common modification applied to beam search scores. Raw log-probabilities favor shorter sequences because each additional token multiplies a probability less than one, adding a negative log-probability. Dividing the cumulative score by the sequence length (or a power of the length, controlled by a length penalty hyperparameter) reduces this bias. Without length normalization, beam search often produces unnaturally short outputs.

Despite its benefits for structured tasks like machine translation and abstractive summarization, beam search is generally not preferred for open-ended text generation. Maximizing probability produces repetitive, safe, and formulaic text — a phenomenon sometimes called the "beam search curse." The most probable continuation of a sentence is often a generic phrase that has appeared many times in training data, not the most interesting or natural one. For open-ended generation, sampling-based methods — with or without truncation — typically produce more diverse and human-like text.

Diverse beam search is a variant that penalizes candidates within the same beam group from being too similar to one another, encouraging the final output list to cover a broader range of plausible completions. This is useful in applications like question generation or paraphrase production where the goal is to return several meaningfully different outputs rather than a ranked list of near-identical sequences.
