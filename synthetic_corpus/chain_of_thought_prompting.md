# Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Chain-of-thought prompting is a technique for eliciting more accurate reasoning from large language models by encouraging them to produce explicit intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer. Rather than asking a model to jump directly from a question to an answer, chain-of-thought prompting guides the model to articulate its reasoning process in natural language — breaking a complex problem into manageable steps — which substantially improves performance on tasks that require arithmetic, logical inference, commonsense reasoning, and multi-step problem solving.

The technique was introduced and systematically studied by Wei et al. in 2022, who demonstrated that prompting a sufficiently large language model with a few examples that include explicit reasoning chains causes it to generate similar reasoning chains for new questions. The improvement was striking: on arithmetic benchmarks like GSM8K, chain-of-thought prompting combined with a 540-billion-parameter model dramatically outperformed much larger models prompted without reasoning steps. Critically, the benefits appeared reliably only in models above roughly 100 billion parameters; smaller models did not benefit and sometimes performed worse.

Few-shot chain-of-thought prompting provides the model with a small number of worked examples before the actual question. Each example consists of the question, a step-by-step reasoning chain, and the final answer. The model, having seen this pattern in the prompt, tends to reproduce it for the target question. The quality of the reasoning chains in the examples matters significantly — clear, logically correct intermediate steps produce better downstream performance than flawed or opaque ones.

Zero-shot chain-of-thought prompting removes the need for hand-crafted examples by simply appending the phrase "Let's think step by step" to the question. Surprisingly, this minimal prompt modification elicits systematic reasoning chains from large models and produces substantial accuracy improvements over standard zero-shot prompting. The instruction appears to activate reasoning behaviors that the model learned during pre-training from mathematical and logical text.

Self-consistency is an extension of chain-of-thought prompting that samples multiple reasoning paths for the same question — using temperature greater than zero — and then selects the most common final answer across all samples. Because different reasoning chains may arrive at the same correct answer even when they make different intermediate errors, the majority vote tends to be more reliable than any single chain. Self-consistency consistently outperforms single-sample chain-of-thought across most benchmarks at the cost of additional inference compute.

The limitations of chain-of-thought prompting include its sensitivity to the phrasing of the reasoning examples, its dependence on model scale, and the fact that models can produce plausible-sounding but incorrect reasoning chains. A model may confidently articulate a wrong argument while arriving at a wrong answer, making it difficult to detect errors from the reasoning chain alone.
