# Model Alignment

Model alignment refers to the challenge of ensuring that AI systems behave in accordance with human intentions, values, and goals — particularly as those systems become more capable. An aligned model does what its designers and users actually want, rather than what it was literally trained to do, and it avoids harmful or deceptive behaviors even when doing so might maximize a narrowly defined objective. Alignment is considered one of the central open problems in AI safety research, with its importance growing as models become more powerful and are deployed in high-stakes settings.

The alignment problem arises in part from the difficulty of specifying human values formally. Training objectives like next-token prediction or reward maximization capture some of what we want but are imperfect proxies for the richer, contextual, and often contradictory values humans hold. A model optimizing for a simple reward signal may find unintended ways to satisfy the literal objective while violating its spirit — a phenomenon called reward hacking or specification gaming. An RL agent rewarded for a high game score might discover an exploit rather than learning to play skillfully.

The "helpful, harmless, and honest" framework has been widely adopted as an operational definition of alignment for conversational AI systems. Helpfulness means the model provides genuinely useful assistance; harmlessness means it avoids producing outputs that could cause real-world harm; and honesty means it does not generate false information, is transparent about its limitations, and does not deceive or manipulate users. These three goals are sometimes in tension — a model trained to be maximally helpful might produce harmful content if asked, while a model trained to be overly cautious may refuse legitimate requests.

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is the primary practical technique for improving alignment in large language models. By training a reward model to capture human preferences and then using RL to optimize the language model's outputs according to that reward, RLHF steers the model away from harmful, dishonest, or unhelpful behaviors. However, RLHF is limited by the quality and consistency of human judgments, the coverage of the preference data, and the risk that the reward model itself misaligns with true human values.

Constitutional AI (CAI), developed by Anthropic, is an alternative approach in which a model is trained using a set of written principles — a "constitution" — rather than relying entirely on human feedback. The model is first trained to critique and revise its own outputs according to these principles (a self-supervision step), then fine-tuned using RL with a reward model trained on these self-critiques. This approach reduces the volume of human feedback required while providing more explicit and auditable behavioral constraints.

Scalable oversight is a research direction addressing how humans can supervise AI systems that may eventually become more capable than humans in specific domains. Proposed approaches include debate (where two AI systems argue different positions and a human judges which argument is more compelling), amplification (where human judgment is iteratively supplemented by AI assistance), and process-based supervision (where humans evaluate reasoning steps rather than just final outcomes).
