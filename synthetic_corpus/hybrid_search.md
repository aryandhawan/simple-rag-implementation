# Hybrid Search

Hybrid search is a retrieval strategy that combines dense vector-based semantic search with sparse keyword-based lexical search to improve recall and relevance across a wider range of query types. Neither method alone is optimal for all queries: dense retrieval excels at capturing semantic similarity and handling paraphrase, while sparse retrieval excels at matching exact terms, rare keywords, and technical vocabulary. Hybrid search systems fuse the results of both approaches to leverage the complementary strengths of each.

Dense retrieval, the foundation of semantic search, encodes both queries and documents as continuous embedding vectors and retrieves the nearest neighbors in embedding space. This method captures meaning: a query for "myocardial infarction" will retrieve documents about "heart attack" even if neither phrase appears in the other's vocabulary, because the embeddings of medically related phrases are geometrically close. However, dense retrieval can struggle with queries containing rare technical terms, product codes, or named entities that may not have been well-represented in the embedding model's training data.

Sparse retrieval methods, primarily BM25, operate on inverted indices of term frequencies. They score documents by counting how often the query terms appear in the document, weighted by how rare those terms are across the corpus. This makes sparse retrieval highly reliable for exact keyword matching and robust to terms the embedding model has never seen. The limitation is that it is entirely vocabulary-dependent: a query for "heart attack" will not retrieve documents about "myocardial infarction" unless both phrases co-occur, because the method has no notion of semantic equivalence.

Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) is the most widely used fusion method for hybrid search. It combines the ranked lists from each retrieval system by computing a fused score for each document based on its rank position in each list: score(d) = Σ 1 / (k + rank(d, i)), where the sum is over all retrieval systems i and k is a constant (typically 60) that dampens the influence of very high rank positions. RRF is parameter-free and robust to the different score scales of the two systems, making it a practical default.

Weighted linear combination of scores is a simpler alternative that directly combines the normalized relevance scores from dense and sparse retrieval: final_score = α × dense_score + (1 − α) × sparse_score. The weight α must be tuned on a validation set, but the resulting system can be more accurate than RRF when the right balance between the two systems is known in advance.

Hybrid search is particularly valuable for enterprise search over heterogeneous corpora that mix general knowledge documents with specialized technical content. Product catalogs, code repositories, scientific literature, and legal databases all contain a mix of semantic content and precise terminology that benefits from both retrieval strategies. Most production vector databases — including Weaviate, Elasticsearch, and Qdrant — natively support hybrid search pipelines.
