# Transfer Learning

Transfer learning is a machine learning paradigm in which knowledge acquired by a model while solving one task is reused as the starting point for a different but related task. Rather than training a model from random initialization for every new problem, transfer learning allows practitioners to leverage representations developed on large, general datasets and adapt them to specific applications with far less data and compute. It has become the dominant training strategy in computer vision, natural language processing, and increasingly in other modalities.

The theoretical motivation for transfer learning rests on the observation that many tasks share common underlying structure. In NLP, understanding grammar, syntax, word meaning, and factual knowledge are prerequisites for nearly any downstream task — from sentiment analysis to machine translation to question answering. A model pre-trained on a large text corpus absorbs this general knowledge into its weights, creating a rich initialization that reflects the structure of language. Fine-tuning on a small labeled dataset then specializes these representations for the target task, often achieving better performance than training on the labeled data alone from scratch.

The pre-train/fine-tune paradigm is the most common form of transfer learning in modern NLP. A large model — such as BERT, RoBERTa, or a GPT-class model — is first pre-trained on massive unlabeled text using a self-supervised objective like masked language modeling or causal language modeling. This pre-trained model is then fine-tuned on a downstream task by training on labeled examples, either updating all parameters or a subset.

Feature extraction is an alternative transfer learning strategy in which the pre-trained model's weights are completely frozen, and only a small task-specific head added on top is trained. This approach is computationally cheaper because it avoids back-propagating gradients through the large pre-trained backbone, but it is typically less effective than full fine-tuning because the frozen representations may not align perfectly with the target task. Feature extraction is most appropriate when the source and target domains are highly similar or when compute is severely constrained.

Domain adaptation is a transfer learning scenario where the source and target domains differ significantly — for example, transferring from general web text to medical literature or legal contracts. In such cases, an intermediate domain-adaptive pre-training step on unlabeled domain-specific text (before task-specific fine-tuning) can substantially improve results by shifting the model's representations toward the target domain's vocabulary and style.

Negative transfer occurs when the transferred knowledge actively harms performance on the target task, typically when source and target domains are too dissimilar. A model pre-trained on English text that is transferred to a completely different modality without careful adaptation may exhibit negative transfer. Diagnosing and preventing negative transfer requires evaluating whether the transferred model outperforms a task-specific model trained from random initialization on the target data.
